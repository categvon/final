TECHNICAL SKILLS & TRAINING

Entry-Level Expectations & Preparation

This technical skill snapshot highlights the skills needed for entry-level roles in public-facing special collections, where everyday technical decisions shape how collections are cared for and used. It reflects behind-the-scenes work that supports access, including processing and description, digitization, metadata creation, and visitor services, and shows how this labor influences how people encounter collections. The snapshot also points to emerging areas such as digital asset management, introductory coding, and community-centered archival practices, signaling how expectations for archival work continue to shift.

Skills & Preparation

technical skills

Collections Care
Entry-level workers are expected to support inventory, care, and basic management of collection materials.

Archival Processing
Familiarity with finding aids and archival description is necessary to support access to archival collections.

Digitization
Entry-level staff should understand how digitization and item selection support access, even if they are not leading digitization projects.

Metadata
Workers are expected to understand how descriptive information enables discovery and use.

Visitor Services
Technical work is closely tied to patron access and service rather than being purely backend.

Skills Identified as Growing in Importance

Digital Asset Management Systems (DAMS) & Digital Collections Literacy
Growing expectations for familiarity with DAMS and with digital collections workflows that connect assets, metadata, and access platforms.

Coding and scripting (e.g., Python)
These skills are becoming more common in training, though not universally required.

Community archives practices
Skills that support community-based collections are identified as an area of growth.
