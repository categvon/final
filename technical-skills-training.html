Entry-level work in public library special collections focuses on hands-on technical tasks that support access, care, and discovery. Early-career professionals contribute to established workflows rather than leading projects independently.

Technical Skill Snapshot
Entry-level roles commonly involve collections management and stewardship, including inventory and care of physical and digital materials. Familiarity with archival processing—such as arrangement, description, and finding aids—is essential to support access to archival collections. Staff are also expected to understand how digitization and item selection contribute to access, and how descriptive metadata enables discovery and use. Technical work in special collections is closely tied to visitor services and public access.

Skills Identified as Growing in Importance
Job postings and professional literature increasingly emphasize digital collections literacy, including familiarity with digital asset management systems (DAMS) and workflows that connect assets, metadata, and access platforms. Introductory coding or scripting skills, such as Python, are becoming more common in training, though they are not universally required. Community archives practices that support community-based collections are also an emerging area of growth.

Professional Job Listings & Preferred Skills
Recent postings illustrate how these expectations appear in practice. An Accessioning Archivist role at UCLA Library Special Collections emphasizes accessioning and stewardship of physical and digital archival collections, managing new acquisitions and backlogs, maintaining documentation, and collaborating with collections staff. A Special Collections Archivist Librarian position at the Denver Public Library focuses on arranging and describing materials in multiple formats, supporting preservation workflows, and providing reference and outreach services for public users.

Training Agenda
Preparation for entry into special collections work typically includes archival studies coursework focused on appraisal, arrangement, description, and access, paired with hands-on experience. Relevant coursework includes Seminar: Special Collections, Archival Description and Access Systems, Metadata, and Digital Asset Management. Fieldwork in libraries or information organizations is critical for translating coursework into job-ready experience.

Internships and Experiential Learning
Experiential learning further supports preparation for the field. The Library of Congress Junior Fellows Program offers paid summer internships working with physical and digital collections, providing experience in archival processing, description, preservation, reference, access, and digitization. Nonprofit opportunities such as the GLBT Historical Society’s volunteer and internship program provide hands-on experience with archival arrangement and description, metadata creation, digitization, born-digital materials, and researcher access in a community-centered archive.
