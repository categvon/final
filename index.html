RESEARCH & SPECIAL COLLECTIONS LIBRARIAN

Los Angeles Public Library

This website presents a comprehensive specialization dossier examining a professional role within the field of Information Studies. The dossier integrates interview insights, secondary research, labor analysis, technical skill requirements, and reflections on the future impact of AI.

Overview

Ani Boyadjian’s work shows that special collections in a public library is about far more than rare books. It is about stewardship, access, systems, and the ongoing work of making community history visible and usable. As Research and Special Collections Manager at the Los Angeles Public Library’s Central Library, Boyadjian has helped expand digitization, reopen the Rare Books Room, support archival acquisitions, and build infrastructures that connect preservation to public service.

What stands out most in her account is how many kinds of labor converge in this role. Special collections work includes preservation, cataloging, metadata creation, digitization, oral history, grant writing, exhibitions, and researcher support. In other words, this field is not only about caring for materials after they arrive. It is also about building the systems, descriptions, funding streams, and workflows that make those materials accessible in the first place.

Boyadjian also emphasizes that technology is now central to the profession. Her experience with ContentDM, metadata crosswalks, digital asset management, and oral history platforms like TheirStory shows that special collections increasingly depends on technical fluency. At the same time, she makes clear that technology does not replace professional judgment. Automated systems can help with transcription or access, but they do not eliminate the labor of description, review, consent, preservation, or relationship-building.

For MLIS students, this specialization is worth attention because it combines public service, cultural stewardship, and infrastructure work. It is a field shaped by community histories, institutional constraints, and evolving digital practices. Boyadjian’s guiding philosophy captures that ethic well: we are only passing through these collections, and our task is to leave them in better condition, and with better access, than we found them.
