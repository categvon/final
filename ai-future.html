AI & THE FUTURE

Impact on Professional Practice

Automation, augmentation, and long-term transformation.

Ani Boyadjian’s view of AI is careful, open-ended, and grounded in practice. Rather than treating AI as either a solution or a threat, she describes it as something libraries are only beginning to understand. In her view, AI is already affecting library work, but the larger ethical and institutional implications remain unresolved.

One part of that future is already visible in tools like TheirStory, which can automatically transcribe oral history interviews and make parts of the workflow faster. But Boyadjian is clear that this kind of automation does not replace the real labor of special collections work. Staff still have to research interviewees, build trust, review transcripts, correct mistakes, manage consent, and preserve context. AI may make some tasks more efficient, but it does not remove the human work that makes archives meaningful and responsible.

Future Outlook

The more difficult issue is authenticity. As born-digital materials become more common, libraries will increasingly face questions about whether files have been altered or generated with AI. Even trusted donations may contain materials whose origins are not immediately clear. For special collections, that creates new challenges around provenance, metadata, and the credibility of the archive itself.

Our takeaway is that AI will likely intensify the responsibilities of the field rather than make them disappear. Future special collections work will require stronger policies, clearer documentation practices, and ongoing institutional conversation about ethics and standards. AI may become part of the infrastructure, but the future of the profession will still depend on human judgment, transparency, and care.
