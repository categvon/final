ECOLOGICAL LANDSCAPE

Interview & Analysis

Interview Insights

Introduce yourself and your role

Timestamp — 0:26

Ani Boyadjian is the Research and Special Collections Manager at the Los Angeles Public Library’s Central Library, a role she has held since 2013. She has worked in the LAPL system since 1997 and is a UCLA alumna, completing both her undergraduate and graduate degrees there.

Special Collections at Central Library has existed for decades, but the collections expanded significantly after the 1986 Central Library fire. The subsequent “Save the Books” campaign raised funds that allowed the library to acquire additional rare and special materials. Boyadjian also highlights the role of librarian Romaine Ahlstrom, who stewarded and guided the development of Special Collections.

The library’s Special Collections Reading Room is named after John Brockman, who supported the collections in the 1970s. When Boyadjian began her position in 2013, however, the reading room had been closed due to staffing shortages. Researchers viewed rare materials in the Art Department while staff retrieved items from Special Collections stacks.

Reopening the Rare Books Room for appointments became one of her first priorities. To support this work, Boyadjian proposed creating a new unit focused on digitization and special collections management. Having previously worked as a cataloger for sixteen years—primarily on the technical and metadata side of library systems—she brought the library’s digitization unit with her into the new department.

This expansion allowed the team to hire four additional staff members dedicated to digitizing special collections materials. Today, the department holds approximately 22,000 monographs, alongside specialized materials such as artist books, travel posters, citrus crate labels, California prints, Japanese prints, and book arts.

Special Collections at LAPL extend beyond a single department. The Central Library also maintains a photograph archive of more than 3.5 million images and a map collection containing hundreds of thousands of maps, while smaller special collections continue to be discovered across branch libraries throughout the system.

Typical training backgrounds

Timestamp — 4:32

Because the Los Angeles Public Library operates within a civil service hiring system, most staff enter the organization as librarians rather than archivists. The library does not currently have a formal archivist classification, though many employees working in Special Collections have backgrounds in archival studies or digital asset management.

Much of the department’s work focuses on collection stewardship and preservation. For example, the library holds an extensive menu collection of more than 17,000 items, which was not properly housed when Boyadjian began her role. The team rehoused the menus, created an inventory, and began identifying materials for digitization.

In addition to rare books and special materials, the department also manages archival collections. One of the most significant recent acquisitions is the Brockman Gallery Archive. The Brockman Gallery, founded in Leimert Park in the 1960s by Dale and Alonzo Davis, was one of the first galleries in Los Angeles to provide space for artists of color.

When Dale Davis donated the archive to LAPL, he explained that his goal in founding the gallery was simple:

“I wanted a person of color walking by the gallery to see something magical happening inside and know that they belong there.”

The gallery had a major influence on the Los Angeles art world, supporting artists such as Charles White and David Hammons. Today the archive is frequently loaned to museums and exhibitions, and institutions such as MOCA and LACMA continue to feature the gallery’s history.

A typical day in Special Collections might include assisting researchers in the reading room, caring for fragile materials, cataloging collections, creating finding aids, and preparing items for digitization.

Is Special Collections a growing field?

Timestamp — 8:37

While public libraries historically have not had the same depth of special collections as academic institutions, Boyadjian believes the field is becoming increasingly important in the public library context.

Public libraries function as community hubs, and special collections allow institutions to reflect and preserve the histories of the communities they serve. Community archives and locally focused collections are becoming a growing area of interest.

She also notes that many professionals in the field are approaching retirement, which may create opportunities for new librarians and archivists. At the same time, archival work often relies on short-term contracts and project-based positions, which can offer less stability than civil service roles in public libraries or permanent positions in academic institutions.

What technical skills and programs are important for your role?

Timestamp — 10:37

Boyadjian notes that recent graduates entering library work often have stronger technical and digital asset management skills than earlier generations of librarians.

Her own career began in cataloging, which provided extensive experience with metadata and working with programmers and web developers. That background proved valuable as the library expanded its digital collections infrastructure.

For example, the library migrated its photo database from MARC records stored in a separate system into the digital collections platform ContentDM. The project required an extensive metadata crosswalk, mapping fields between systems—a process that took nearly a year to complete.

Today the department is also exploring long-term digital preservation solutions and is considering adopting Preservica as a digital repository for its growing collection of digital assets.

Library staff continue to develop technical skills through professional development opportunities such as Society of American Archivists certifications, conferences like the Digital Library Federation Forum, and training in programming languages such as Python. LAPL also maintains a training budget that supports staff learning and continuing education.

Can you tell us about the oral history platform, TheirStory, that you use at LAPL and why you chose this software?

Timestamp — 10:28

LAPL adopted the oral history platform TheirStory to help formalize and expand the library’s oral history program.

The library had previously conducted interviews—particularly through the Shades of L.A. project in the 1990s—but Boyadjian wanted to build a more structured program focusing on three areas: community stories, institutional history, and archival context for donated collections.

The platform allows the library to conduct remote interviews and automatically generate transcripts, but Boyadjian emphasizes that oral history work remains highly labor-intensive. Interviews require relationship building, background research, and careful review of automated transcripts to correct names, remove filler words, and ensure accuracy.

For example, she interviewed muralist David Botello, who was involved with the East Los Streetscapers and whose work had been featured in a library exhibition. Preparing for the interview required researching his artistic career and community work before recording the conversation.

TheirStory also allows the library to host collections from smaller organizations that lack long-term preservation infrastructure. One example is an upcoming project involving ACT UP Los Angeles, an AIDS activist organization that conducted nearly one hundred oral history interviews documenting activism during the HIV/AIDS crisis.

For Boyadjian, this hosting role reflects an important responsibility for public libraries: serving as stable repositories for community histories that might otherwise be lost.

Is Special Collections a growing field?

Timestamp — 8:37

Boyadjian explains that while special collections have traditionally been associated with academic libraries, they are becoming increasingly important within public libraries as well. Public institutions often serve as community hubs, and special collections allow libraries to preserve and share materials that document local histories and cultural movements.

At the same time, she notes that many professionals in the field are approaching retirement, which may create opportunities for new librarians and archivists. However, archival work can sometimes involve short-term project positions or contract work, particularly outside of civil service systems.

Because of this, public library positions can provide greater long-term stability compared to some archival roles, while still offering opportunities to work with rare materials, archives, and community history projects.

Can you tell us about the oral history platform TheirStory and why LAPL chose it?

Timestamp — 10:28

The Los Angeles Public Library recently adopted TheirStory, an online platform designed to facilitate oral history interviews and automatically generate transcripts. While LAPL had previously conducted oral history work—particularly through the Shades of L.A. project in the 1990s—Boyadjian wanted to develop a more structured and sustainable oral history program.

The new program focuses on three main areas: documenting community stories, recording institutional history from retiring library staff, and conducting contextual interviews connected to archival collections donated to the library.

Although the platform helps streamline recording and transcription, Boyadjian notes that oral history work still requires significant preparation. Interviewers must research the interviewee, develop thoughtful questions, and carefully edit transcripts to correct names and remove filler language. The technology helps manage the recordings, but the intellectual and relational work of oral history remains essential.

How does LAPL collaborate with community archives and organizations?

Timestamp — 20:40

Boyadjian describes how public libraries can act as stewards for community histories, especially when smaller organizations lack long-term digital preservation infrastructure.

For example, LAPL is working with ACT UP Los Angeles, an AIDS activist organization that conducted nearly one hundred oral history interviews documenting activism during the HIV/AIDS crisis. By hosting and preserving these interviews, the library helps ensure that the materials remain accessible to researchers and the public.

Projects like this demonstrate how public libraries can serve as stable repositories for community archives, helping preserve historical documentation that might otherwise be difficult for smaller groups to maintain.

How are Special Collections projects funded?

Timestamp — 25:30

Funding for special collections work often comes from a combination of municipal budgets, grants, and donor support. Many projects at LAPL are supported through the Library Foundation of Los Angeles, which acts as the fiscal agent for grants and philanthropic funding.

Boyadjian notes that some essential work—such as cataloging, metadata creation, and archival processing—can be difficult to fund because it is less visible than exhibitions or digitization projects. As a result, these tasks are often incorporated into larger grant proposals.

The department currently participates in several grant-funded initiatives, including projects supported by the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation, which focus on oral histories, community archiving, and documentation of environmental and cultural histories.

What challenges come with working in a public institution?

Timestamp — 29:30

Working within a public institution like the Los Angeles Public Library means following formal city procurement and administrative procedures. Purchases, contracts, and services often require competitive bidding or formal justification.

For example, when arranging for USC to digitize historical volumes of the Library Board of Commissioners minutes, Boyadjian had to demonstrate why that institution was the most appropriate vendor for the project.

While these processes ensure transparency and accountability for public funds, they can also slow down projects compared to the more flexible systems used by private institutions.

How do exhibitions and programming connect to Special Collections?

Timestamp — 33:00

In addition to managing Special Collections, Boyadjian also serves as the exhibitions liaison for Central Library. The building contains three gallery spaces that host exhibitions connected to library collections, local history, and community themes.

Exhibitions are often planned several years in advance, similar to museum schedules. These projects allow the library to highlight materials from its collections while making them accessible to broader audiences.

Programming is another important component of the library’s work. Across the LAPL system, thousands of programs are hosted each month, including lectures, workshops, and performances. These events help connect the library’s collections with the communities they represent.

How might AI affect library and archival work?

Timestamp — 41:00

Artificial intelligence is beginning to influence areas of library work such as transcription, data analysis, and digital collections management. Tools that automatically generate transcripts can help streamline workflows, particularly for oral history projects.

However, Boyadjian notes that AI also introduces new challenges. Archivists and librarians must consider how to identify and preserve born-digital materials that may include AI-generated content, and how to verify authenticity in digital records.

As these technologies evolve, libraries will likely continue developing policies and ethical guidelines for managing and preserving AI-related materials.

Reflections on career paths in libraries and archives

Timestamp — 44:30

Reflecting on her career, Boyadjian explains that she did not originally enter the field with formal training in special collections or archives. Instead, she built her expertise over time through hands-on experience and professional development opportunities such as California Rare Book School.

Her career demonstrates how library work often involves continuous learning and adaptation, particularly as technology and research needs change.

“You don’t own anything—you’re just passing through. The goal is to leave collections in a better condition than when you found them.”
